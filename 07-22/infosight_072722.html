Proposed Rule on Reportable Cyber Incidents Approved by Board

The NCUA Board has issued a proposed rule that would require a federally insured credit union (FICU) to notify the NCUA as soon as possible but no later than 72 hours after they reasonably believe that a reportable cyber incident has occurred.

Under the proposed rule, a FICU would be required to report a cyber incident that leads to a substantial loss of confidentiality, integrity, or availability of a member information system because of the exposure of sensitive data, disruption of vital member services, or that has a serious impact on the safety and resiliency of operational systems and processes.

Comments on the proposed rule must be received no later than 60 days following publication in the Federal Register.

Fed Proposes Default Rates Rule for LIBOR Contracts

The Federal Reserve Board recently announced it is seeking comment on a proposal that provides default rules for certain contracts that use the LIBOR reference rate, which will be discontinued next year. The proposal implements the Adjustable Interest Rate (LIBOR) Act (Pub. L. 117-103, div. U), which Congress enacted on March 15, 2022. In response to the planned end of LIBOR by June 30, 2023, Congress enacted the LIBOR Act to provide a uniform, nationwide solution for replacing references to LIBOR in existing contracts without adequate fallback provisions, which are provisions in the contract related to the identification of an alternative reference rate.

The proposal would replace references to LIBOR in certain contracts with the applicable Board-selected replacement rate after June 30, 2023. The contracts include those governed by domestic law that do not mature before LIBOR ends and that lack adequate fallback provisions. The proposal identifies separate Board-selected replacement rates for derivatives transactions, contracts where a government-sponsored enterprise is a party, and all other affected contracts. As required by the law, each proposed replacement rate is based on the Secured Overnight Financing Rate.

Comments on the proposed rule rule will be accepted for 30 days after publication in the Federal Register.

Questions and Answers

Q: What is an electronic fund transfer?

A: It is a transaction initiated through an electronic terminal, telephone, computer or magnetic tape that alerts a financial institution to credit or debit a consumer's asset account, such as a share or share draft account.

Q: When must a credit union comply with Reg. E?

A: A credit union must comply with Reg. E whenever it offers its members an electronic fund transfer service covered by the EFTA

Q: A member recently reported the loss of their ATM card and subsequent unauthorized use. They had their pin # written on the back of the card. Can the credit union hold the member liable for the total amount of the unauthorized transactions?

A: Negligence by the consumer cannot be used as the basis for imposing greater liability than is permissible under Regulation E.

If the member notifies the credit union within two business days of discovering a loss or theft of an access device (ATM card), the member is only liable for the lesser of $50 or the amount of money or property obtained through unauthorized use before notification to the credit union.

If notification takes more than two business days after the member learned of the loss or theft, the member may become liable for not more than the lesser of $500 or the sum of 1) the lesser of $50 or the amount of unauthorized transfers that occur within the two business days, and 2) the amount of unauthorized transfers that occur after two business days and before notification, if the credit union can establish that it would have prevented additional losses had the member provided timely notification.

NOTE: Credit unions can implement a policy to terminate any access device if a PIN # is found written on it. However, the credit union should not force repayment of the loss to reactivate an ATM card. It could be considered a violation of Regulation E liability provisions.

Q: Must a credit union follow through on its investigation of an electronic funds transfer (EFT) error if the member withdraws their complaint?

A: No. The credit union has no further error-resolution responsibilities under Regulation E if the member voluntarily withdraws their notice alleging an EFT error, such as:

  • Unauthorized and incorrect EFTs to or from a member's account;
  • The omission of an EFT from a periodic statement;
  • The credit union’s computational or bookkeeping error; and/or
  • The member’s receipt of an incorrect amount from an electronic terminal.

The member may need to reassert the error allegation unless the credit union already had complied with all of Reg E’s error resolution requirements before the allegation was withdrawn. However, the member must reassert the error allegation within the 60-day period after receiving the periodic statement containing the error.
