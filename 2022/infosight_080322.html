FATF: Risk-based Approach for Real Estate Sector

The Financial Action Task Force recently published updated "Guidance for a Risk-Based Approach — Real Estate Sector." FATF assessments show that the real estate sector often has poor understanding of the risks that criminal may use real estate in their illicit activities or to launder their criminal profits, and regularly fails to mitigate them. The revised "Risk-Based Approach Guidance for the Real Estate Sector" highlights the importance for the sector to increase its understanding of the money laundering and terrorist financing risks it faces. Vulnerabilities include exploitation by politically exposed persons, the purchase of luxury real estate, the use of virtual assets, the use of anonymous companies and gatekeepers as instruments to launder the proceeds of crime.

Questions and Answers

Q: If a member adds her husband as a joint owner on her share account, does that make the husband a member and is he now entitled to receive loans from the credit union? Some individuals believe that if they are a joint owner they're automatically a member.

A: A member may establish a joint account with anyone the member chooses, regardless of whether that person is a member. Joint ownership is not membership. It does not entitle the individual to membership privileges such as borrowing, serving on the credit union board or committees, or voting at the annual meeting. If the joint owner is in the field of membership they may become a member of the credit union by completing a separate membership card. Such person should also pay any membership fee and be approved for membership in the usual manner. A joint owner who is a member may want to establish a separate individual account. The non-member joint owner has full access to the funds as described on the account card unless there are specific restrictions provided in the account agreement.

Q: We’ve stressed getting complete information on membership and account cards in order to prevent the credit union from being involved in disputes. However, disputes still arise, especially in divorce cases. What can we do to deal with these situations?

A: One of the most common types of disputes results from a member’s failure to remove funds from accounts held jointly with former spouses. Very often, the family of a deceased member will be surprised to find a former spouse listed as a joint owner on the member’s share account. The Personal Representative of the member may seek to withdraw funds, although the joint account contract would not allow it.

The credit union should not permit itself to be put in the middle of such disputes. In these types of cases, the credit union should simply refuse to honor any withdrawals. The credit union should then turn the matter over to the credit union’s attorney, who will submit it to the courts for a decision. A court proceeding called “interpleader” has been developed specifically to deal with such situations.

Q: Is a person automatically a member if they are a joint account owner?

A: Credit union members may establish individual or joint accounts. A member may set up a joint account with anyone the member chooses – member or non-member, adult or minor. Joint ownership is not membership. If a joint account holder is within the field of membership, they may choose to become a member of the credit union by signing up, paying the membership fee (if any) and being approved for membership in the usual manner. A joint owner who is not a member may not establish a separate individual account; however, a joint owner who is a member may elect to establish a separate account. Though most credit unions’ core systems require it, it is not necessary to open an individual (or “primary”) account to establish membership. Membership may be established by depositing the required share in either an individual or joint account.

Q: How should our member services department respond when a member says: “I don’t have a will; should I have all of my accounts in joint ownership so that my estate won’t be probated?” Should we provide legal advice or tax advice?

A: One risk that is never warranted is providing legal or tax advice to members. Opening a deposit account involves establishing a legal relationship between the member and the credit union. The member’s inquiry about avoiding probate by establishing joint ownership clearly goes beyond the credit union staff’s area of expertise.

Respond to the member by stating that you cannot provide estate planning advice or any other sort of ownership or ownership-transfer advice. Credit union staff may state that the credit union can establish various types of accounts, and that the member (or their attorney) can determine whether one or more of them will meet their needs. Keep in mind that while joint account laws typically protect the credit union in dealing with any owner (absent notice of a dispute or a contract requirement to get the consent of more than one owner for a transaction), how a court will treat the situation as between the various owners, estates, etc. with an interest can be quite uncertain.
