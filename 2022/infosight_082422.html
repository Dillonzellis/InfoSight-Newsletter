Questions and Answers

Q: True or false: All credit unions that charge fees for paying overdrafts are required to disclose them on the periodic statement, both for the statement period and for the year-to-date.

A: True. The Federal Reserve Board and NCUA Truth in Savings (TIS) regulations require financial institutions that promote the payment of overdrafts to disclose on the periodic statement the fees charged for overdraft services and the fees charged for returning items unpaid, both for the statement period and for the year-to-date.

The Fed and NCUA recently amended these rules to expand the disclosure requirements to all financial institutions regulated under the Fed's Regulation DD or NCUA's Part 707, regardless of whether they promote the payment of overdrafts. This disclosure does not have to be provided if no fees have been charged (e.g., the credit union would not have to disclose "$0" for the statement period or year-to-date).

The disclosure must be made in a tabular format and be near the other itemized fees. The final rule includes a sample form for making this disclosure. Credit unions may either use the term "returned item fee" or "NSF fee" on the form to describe the fee for returning items unpaid.

Q: Does federal law require credit unions to provide an overdraft notice when an NSF item has been presented against a member’s account, whether it is paid or returned?

A: No. Although most financial institutions provide overdraft notices as a matter of industry practice, providing the notice is simply a courtesy, not a requirement. Providing the notice may also be considered as a loss control measure, since it serves as a red flag for errors and unauthorized items. However, there is no federal law or regulation requiring that the credit union provide the notice.

Please keep in mind that NCUA’s Part 701.21(c)(3) on credit applications and overdrafts requires a federal credit union to have a written overdraft policy if it advances money to a member to cover an account deficit (so-called courtesy pay programs). The policy must address caps on overdraft lending, time limits on repayment, per-member overdraft limits, overdraft fees, and interest rates, if any. Without such a policy, the credit union would be required to keep a credit application on file for the member before advancing such funds.

Q: There's a 45-day rule in the NCUA rules that says a member has to either deposit funds or get a loan from the credit union to cover an overdraft. If they don't do either within that time, does the credit union have to write off the amount?

A: Section 701.21(c)(3) of NCUA's regulation requires credit unions to have a written overdraft policy that:

  1. Sets a cap on the total dollar amount of all overdrafts the credit union will honor consistent with the credit union’s ability to absorb losses;
  2. Establishes a time limit not to exceed 45 calendar days for a member either to deposit funds or to obtain an approved loan from the credit union to cover an overdraft; limits the dollar amount of overdrafts the credit union will honor per member; and
  3. Establishes the fee and interest rate, if any, the credit union will charge members for honoring overdrafts.

NCUA issued a Letter to Federally Insured Credit Unions, 05-CU-03 which includes joint interagency guidance on overdraft protection programs. The guidance states:

“In addition, overdraft balances should generally be charged off when considered uncollectible, but no later than 60 days from the date first overdrawn. In some cases, an institution may allow a consumer to cover an overdraft through an extended repayment plan when the consumer is unable to bring the account to a positive balance within the required time frames. The existence of the repayment plan, however, would not extend the charge-off determination period beyond 60 days (or shorter period if applicable) as measured from the date of the overdraft. Any payments received after the account is charged off (up to the amount charged off against allowance) should be reported as a recovery.”
