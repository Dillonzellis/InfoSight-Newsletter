Training at Your Fingertips

Two-Day Virtual BSA Workshop is Coming in May!
Join the LSCU League Compliance and Regulatory Services team along with some surprise guest speakers for a two-day BSA Workshop on May 4th (Day 1) and May 5th (Day 2), 2022. During this exciting training, we will cover BSA requirements and discuss how it applies to everyday credit union operations.

BSA training is a core requirement of a satisfactory Bank Secrecy Act and Anti-Money Laundering (BSA/AML) compliance program. At a minimum, a BSA/AML training program must provide training for all personnel whose duties require knowledge of the BSA. Since there is some flexibility in how to design a training program, our training is broken down into two days, allowing your credit union to determine whether employees should attend one or both days of training. Attending this workshop will meet the annual training requirements under BSA, so feel free to sign up as many employees as you need to.

On Day One (BSA: What All Employees Need to Know), we will go over the basics of BSA training requirements under the regulations and apply that learning to specific jobs in your credit union such as, tellers, member service representatives, compliance staff, lenders, etc.

On Day Two (BSA: What BSA Officers Need to Know), is geared more towards BSA officers. We will cover a variety of topics like how to provide information on SARs to help build a case and how information is used to prosecute that case, what are the most common exam findings are when it comes to BSA, and how to handle banking “legal” marijuana businesses.

CU Action Update

Risk Alert (22-RISK-01) Heightened Risk of Social Engineering and Phishing Attacks
The on-going conflict in Ukraine has raised concerns about potential cyberattacks in the U.S., including those against the financial services sector. Credit unions and vendors, regardless of size, are potential targets for cyberattacks, like social engineering and phishing attacks, and must remain vigilant. Your credit union should report any cyber incidents to the NCUA, your local FBI field office or the Internet Crime Complaint Center, and the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency.

This Risk Alert reminds credit unions of the ongoing threat of social engineering and phishing attacks and reiterates the continued importance of educating your employees and members on how to avoid these threats.

USAA Fined $140M for BSA/AML Violations – What You Can Learn from Their Mistakes
FinCEN and OCC issued enforcement orders against USAA Federal Savings Bank, Austin, Texas, for willful violations of the Bank Secrecy Act and its implementing regulations. USAA FSB admitted that it willfully failed to implement and maintain an anti money laundering (AML) program that met the minimum requirements of the BSA from at least January 2016 through April 2021.

The Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC) assessed a civil penalty of $60 million for related violations. Since many of the facts and circumstances underlying the OCC’s civil penalty also form the basis of FinCEN’s Consent Order, USAA FSB will pay a total of $140 million to the U.S. Treasury for its violations.

Mistakes made by USAA and what can credit unions can do to avoid them:

  • USAA BSA program failed to grow as the bank grew in asset size – As your credit union expands it is important to update all components of your program to address increased risk. This may mean hiring more staff and increased training.
  • USAA failed to make adequate improvements after examiners found deficiencies in their BSA program during a 2018 exam. – Address & correct findings in a timely manner. Even if you cannot completely correct a finding prior to the next exam, it is important to document and be able to show examiners your efforts in working toward mitigation.
  • BSA department was understaffed and relied too heavily on third party vendors to complete compliance functions. – Even when using vendors, it is still the credit union’s responsibility to manage the entire process including having enough staff to monitor system effectiveness, alerts, reports, and the completion of SARs. Each of these components are crucial to the success of your BSA program.
  • USAA did not provide staff with appropriate training, nor did they have processes in place to handle the level of compliance needed. – Investing in your staff is an investment in your credit union. This training must be ongoing to ensure that staff is kept abreast of current trends related to BSA. Regulators recommend training every 12 to 18 months. A properly trained staff can spot weaknesses and recommend improvements to your policies & procedures.
  • Systems in place did not adequately identify suspicious activity, leading to unreviewed alerts, which also leads to a failure to file SARs. - Your core system/processor is only as good as the parameters you have in place based on your risk. Your systems may require frequent testing until you achieve satisfactory results. If your core system is not meeting your expectations, it may be time to reevaluate that relationship.
  • Opening accounts without proper due diligence policies. New accounts not risk rated properly which led to a lack of due diligence in monitoring accounts. - New account checklists are a game changer. Completing one for every new account offers a bird’s eye view of what this new relationship will look like. Your risk rating is determined by the information received from this document. The moment that relationship is not performing as initially stated, a red flag should go up and your next step should be included in your due diligence procedures to determine if a simple conversation is needed, a SAR needs to be completed, or a new risk rating is required.

Industry News

Proposed Update of Policies and Procedures
The Federal Reserve Board on March 22, 2022, invited comments on an interagency proposal to update policies and procedures governing administrative proceedings for supervised financial institutions. The proposed updates would modernize these rules, aligning them with current practices and facilitating the use of electronic communications and technology in administrative proceedings. The proposal was jointly developed with the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency, and the National Credit Union Administration.

In addition, the Federal Reserve Board invited comments on proposed updates to additional rules applicable only to Board administrative proceedings. These proposed updates would codify existing practices and promote transparency.

Comments on the proposed rules will be accepted for 60 days following Federal Register publication.

Bureau Guidance on ‘Gag’ Clauses and Fake Review Fraud
The CFPB issued policy guidance regarding potentially illegal practices related to consumer reviews to ensure that customers can write reviews, particularly ones posted online, about financial products and services that accurately reflect their opinions and experiences. The guidance also highlights those practices such as posting fake reviews or inserting clauses that forbid a customer from publishing an honest review may violate the Consumer Financial Protection Act.

The CFPB’s guidance [Compliance Bulletin 2022-05] describes certain business practices related to customer reviews that are generally unlawful under the Consumer Financial Protection Act. including:

  • Contractual ‘Gag’ Clauses: Attempting to silence consumers from posting an online review can undermine fair competition. Banks and financial companies that include clauses in form contracts that forbid a consumer from posting an honest review may be engaged in unfair or deceptive practices.

DOJ Issues Guidance on ADA Web Accessibility
The Department of Justice recently issued guidance on web accessibility and the Americans with Disabilities Act. In addition to plain-language information designed for those without a legal or technical background, the guidance reviews the department’s ongoing work to advance website accessibility.

The guidance discusses a range of topics, including the importance of web accessibility, barriers that inaccessible websites create for some people with disabilities, when the ADA requires web content to be accessible, tips on making web content accessible and other information and resources.

2021 HMDA Modified LAR Availability
The HMDA modified loan/application register (LAR) is now available for each institution that filed HMDA data collected in 2021. The modified LARs provide each financial institution's loan-level HMDA data, as modified to protect applicant and borrower privacy in accordance with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau’s final policy guidance on the disclosure of HMDA data.

The modified LARs can be accessed here: https://ffiec.cfpb.gov/data-publication/modified-lar.

We encourage financial institutions to direct any questions regarding data processing or publication to HMDAHelp@cfpb.gov.
