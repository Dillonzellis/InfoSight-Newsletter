CU Action Update

CFPB Notes Changes in OD Practices
The CFPB posted a blog article, "Comparing overdraft fees and policies across banks," noting that in recent weeks there has been an increase in changes banks are making to their overdraft programs. The changes vary widely, and include

  • eliminating NSF fees charged when transactions bounce;
  • reducing the size of the overdraft fee;
  • reducing the number of overdraft/NSF fees the bank can charge a customer each day;
  • providing or increasing the amount an account can go negative before charging an overdraft fee;
  • providing a grace period to bring an account back to positive before charging an overdraft fee; and
  • eliminating “extended” or “sustained” overdraft fees charged when an account is not brought back to a positive balance after a certain period of time.

The Bureau provided a snapshot of large banks' overdraft and NSF practices through the first three quarters of 2021 and any changes those banks have announced in those practices.

Within the last few months several credit unions have also eliminated or reduced overdraft and non-sufficient funds (NSF) fees for members. Currently, NSF/OD fees are the number one liability in the industry. It is important that credit unions evaluate their current program for compliance as well as consider alternative options to ODP.

CFPB recently issued guidance on initiatives related to junk fees and OD/NSF fees, stating that that they plan to increase supervisory scrutiny in this area. Don’t wait until the problem arrives at your front door, get a head start and review your program now.

As your compliance team, we want to provide you with necessary resources to help you meet the compliance expectations associated with NSF/OD programs. Below are some tools that you can use to review your program today.

Fed Posts ID Fraud Mitigation Toolkit
The Federal Reserve's FedPayments Improvement group announced a new Synthetic Identity Fraud Mitigation Toolkit.

The toolkit will be expanded from time to time, and provides useful insights and resources for identifying and mitigating synthetic identity fraud, to:

  • Increase education and awareness about synthetic identity fraud
  • Enable the payments industry to better identify and fight synthetic identity fraud
  • Foster payments industry collaboration on synthetic identity fraud mitigation

FinCEN: Beneficial Ownership Reporting and Next Steps
FinCEN issued a Statement Regarding Beneficial Ownership Information Reporting and Next Steps, noting that the comment period for the FinCEN December 8, 2021, notice of proposed rulemaking requiring the reporting of beneficial ownership information (BOI) has closed. FinCEN received over 230 comments.

The Reporting NPRM is the first in a series of rulemakings that FinCEN will issue to implement the Corporate Transparency Act (CTA). The next step in the CTA rulemaking series will be FinCEN’s publication of proposed rules on BOI access and disclosure requirements (the “Access NPRM”), which FinCEN anticipates publishing later this year.

NCUA Capital Adequacy Framework Webinar- Registration Open
Final Risk-Based Capital (RBC) rule effective for January 2022 and a final rule approved by the Board at the December 2021 meeting for the Complex Credit Union Leverage Ratio (CCULR) will transform the regulatory capital framework for the credit union industry. The implementation of RBC and CCULR will require complex credit unions to understand how to evaluate regulatory capital, Risk-Based Capital standards, and how the new Subordinated Debt rule intersect with the Capital standards. Credit unions will also be trained on the Call Reporting requirements for both the RBC and CCULR forms and instructions.

The Capital Adequacy Framework Webinar will take place on Wednesday, Feb 23rd at 2:00 EST where staff from the NCUA’s Office of Examination and Insurance will focus on the overall adequacy framework for all federally insured credit unions, including the final RBC and CCULR rules that are effective this year for complex credit unions. Romance Scams Alert
Five federal agencies—the CFTC, CFPB, DHS/ICE, U.S. Postal Inspection Service, and FinCEN— have issued an alert to remind the public about the ongoing dangers of romance scams.

The agencies have launched Dating or Defrauding?, a national awareness effort to alert the public to romance scams that target victims largely through dating apps or social media. The campaign is supported by USAGov/Outreach, a division of the U.S. General Services Administration's Technology Transformation Services.

Credit unions can help prevent romance scams by watching for unusual member behaviors and providing education and awareness campaigns. Understanding the typical behavior for your members is an effective way to combat fraud. This understanding makes it easier to spot red flags and determine if your member could be making unusually risky transactions. Now more than ever before when so many people are in lockdown and lonely pursuing online relationships is at an all-time high. Providing awareness campaigns can enlighten members who may not even know these types of scams exist.

Use campaigns to share publicly available resources from consumer advocacy groups. The FTC has resources that encourage victims to notify their financial institutions immediately if they believe they are a victim.

Compliance Question of the Week

Q: What is the requirement on sending monthly periodic statements to commercial account holders? Isn't there something in Reg E that requires members to receive statements monthly because if there are electronic transactions that are fraudulent, they only have X number of days to dispute them? Many of our transactions are electronic, but we have a member who wants to see their activity only quarterly.

A: You don't have to agree to meet the member’s request. If you have language in your business deposit agreement that requires business account owners to notify you within 30 or 60 days of any unauthorized or altered items in a statement, you should emphasize that fact when you explain to the member the importance of reviewing their statement monthly.

Q: Are commercial/business accounts covered by Reg. E?

A: The regulation only covers consumer asset accounts established primarily for personal, family or household purposes. [Definition of "account" in §1005.2(b)], and "consumer" is a natural person [§1005.2(e)]. Therefore, commercial or business accounts, including sole proprietorship accounts, are not covered by the regulation.

However, if your credit union provides its standard Regulation E disclosures to a business (without a disclaimer of their application to business accounts), the disclosures MAY arguably contractually obligate you to comply with the disclosures for a commercial or business account, depending on how the courts of the state in which the account is domiciled apply state law.

Q: My credit union just started opening business accounts and now realize that business account are not covered under Truth in Savings. What kind of disclosures should we provide for these accounts?

A: Although the Truth in Savings is not required for business accounts, it is recommended. Below is a list of business account disclosures to consider:

  • Business Account Truth in Savings-not required by regulation but recommended
  • Business Fee Schedule
  • Business Account Agreement
  • Business Account Funds Availability
  • Business Account Electronic Funds Transfer – not required by regulation but recommended
  • Privacy Disclosure
  • Business Debit Card Disclosure
