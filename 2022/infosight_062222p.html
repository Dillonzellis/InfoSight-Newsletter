InfoSight Focus

Content Update: US Savings Bond Validation Tool
A recent RISK Alert from CUNA Mutual Group discusses scams involving the redemption of US Savings Bonds and includes information about validating bonds presented for cashing. The US Savings Bond topic in the Accounts channel of InfoSight now includes this FAQ for additional information:

Is there a way to VALIDATE a bond to make sure it's legitimate?

Yes! Although the TreasuryDirect tool calculates the value, it does NOT:

  • verify whether the member owns the savings bonds;
  • guarantee the serial number you entered is valid;
  • guarantee a savings bond is eligible to be cashed; or
  • create a savings bond based on information that is entered into this tool.

Credit unions should use the Savings Bond Valuation and Verification for Financial Institutions from the U.S. Department of the Treasury’s Bureau of the Fiscal Service to validate savings bond. In order to obtain access, credit unions will need to email sbvv@fiscal.treasury.gov. Once you have access, it appears very easy to use by following the instructions on how to use this tool.

CU Action Update

FFIEC Announces Availability of 2021 Data on Mortgage Lending
The Federal Financial Institutions Examination Council (FFIEC) announced the availability of data on 2021 mortgage lending transactions reported under the Home Mortgage Disclosure Act (HMDA) by 4,338 U.S. financial institutions. Covered institutions include banks, savings associations, credit unions, and mortgage companies.

The HMDA data are the most comprehensive publicly available information on mortgage market activity. The data are used by industry, consumer groups, regulators, and others to assess potential fair lending risks and for other purposes. The data help the public assess how financial institutions are serving the housing needs of their local communities and facilitate federal financial regulators’ fair lending, consumer compliance, and Community Reinvestment Act examinations.

Industry News

Request for Information Regarding Relationship Banking and Customer Service
The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (Bureau or CFPB) is seeking comments from the public related to relationship banking and how consumers can assert the right to obtain timely responses to requests for information about their accounts from banks and credit unions with more than $10 billion in assets, as well as from their affiliates. A 2010 federal law specifies that consumers have rights to obtain timely responses to requests for information about their accounts from large depository institutions. In a recent Request for Information, the CFPB seeks data about, and consumer experiences with, the obstacles that may prevent people from receiving high standards of customer service and high-quality human interactions with their banks or credit unions.

CFPB Studying Banks' OD Programs
The CFPB posted a Bureau Blog article, "Measuring the impact of financial institution overdraft programs on consumers," announcing that the Bureau has been piloting a supervision effort to collect key metrics from some supervised institutions regarding the consumer impact of their overdraft and non-sufficient fund practices. Over 20 institutions have been asked for data on five consumer-impact metrics.

In addition to these metrics, the CFPB is seeking detailed information about entities’ overdraft practices, including how they assess their fees, their grace periods, the dollar thresholds above which fees are assessed, and caps on the number of fees charged per day, or per statement period.

The article states that the Bureau intends to use this information to identify institutions for further examination and review. It also plans to provide feedback to each institution, as well as to share the information with other regulators.

Compliance Q&As

Q: What is succession planning?

A: Succession planning is a key component in preparing for the future of the credit union. It is a systematic process ensuring leadership continuity by helping credit unions prepare for planned or unplanned absences of the CEO, director, or other critical staff positions. An effective succession plan will enable the credit union to keep critical operations going by maintaining stability and continuity of operations, retaining “institutional knowledge,” and minimizing disruption during periods of change. In short, a credit union's strategic plan is not sound without the inclusion of a viable succession plan.

Q: Why is a strategic plan a necessary part of succession planning? Isn’t succession planning about people?

A: Succession planning should include not only planning to replace people, but should be strongly coordinated with organizational planning. It should begin with an understanding of a company’s strategic direction and practices – the credit union’s vision of where it will be going in the future – and it should be tied to the management of all the transitions that the credit union is now facing, or will one day face. The Board, the CEO, and senior management need to anticipate the knowledge, skills, and abilities needed to meet the credit union’s strategic goals.

Q: Should the President/CEO/Manager be the only focus of succession planning?

A: The board should be planning for multiple successions. While the first succession plan developed should be for the CEO role, the board should also plan for the boards’ own succession as well as for other “key” positions that are critical to the success of the credit union.

Q: What “key” or “critical” positions should be included in succession planning?

A: Succession planning should focus on any key position that is critical to the credit union’s operations, including those that are critical to the overall organizational structure of the credit union.

Examples include:

  1. Key tasks that would stop critical action from taking place;
  2. specialized or unique expertise that is difficult to replace; and
  3. a position that may be the only one of its kind in a particular geographical location in which would be difficult for another location to carry out its functions.

Q: Shouldn’t each department within the credit union be responsible for its own succession planning?

A: Continuity works best. Allowing each department to come up with its own unique process for succession planning can be a troublesome and time-consuming endeavor. Credit unions should create a company-wide process that can be used by each individual department.

Q: Is succession planning only a one-time event? Once a succession plan is drafted, why is it necessary to update and/or revise it?

A: The succession plan should be a living, breathing document that is updated at least annually to account for strategic redirections and changes. It is important to think about succession planning as an ongoing process to continually grow and develop the credit union, which includes continual adjustment to strategic redirections and changes, external market changes, and internal training and development.
