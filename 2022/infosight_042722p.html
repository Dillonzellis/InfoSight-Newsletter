CU Action Update

Are You Using the Correct NCUA Advertisement Statement?
Recent Compliance Consultant reviews have had several findings related errors in posting the NCUA’s Advertising statement. As a reminder, Section 740.5 of NCUA’s rules and regulations allows a credit union to choose either of the following options when complying with the regulation.

  1. Statement: “This Credit Union is federally-insured by the National Credit Union Administration.”
  2. Short Statement (no longer requires accompanying “official sign”): “Federally Insured by NCUA;” or “Insured by NCUA;” or
  3. The Official Sign: (note: if the NCUA official sign in the advertisement is so small that the NCUA’s sign and the two lines of small type become indistinct, the credit union should use the NCUA official advertising statement, or the short statement instead.)

Section 740.5(a) requires the official advertising statement be included on an FICU’s website homepage, as well as all its advertisements. Advertisement as defined by the regulation is any medium that is designed to attract public attention or patronage to a product or business. Based on this definition, the NCUA’s rules and regulations require FICU’s to include a statement about share insurance on its main webpage in additional to any other commercial message designed to attract public attention to a product. Although the regulation only mentions the main page, it is important to understand that most webpages are designed to promote a product, which means that the advertisement statement may be required on multiple pages. For this reason, when viewing websites for credit unions we often find the advertising statement on the footer of every page during a review.

While it may be easier to take this approach, FICU’s are not required to include the official advertising statement on every page of the website. Section 740.5(c) offers several exceptions including advertisements that do not relate to member accounts, including but not limited to advertisements relating to loans by the credit union, safekeeping box business or services, traveler’s checks on which the credit union is not primarily liable, and credit life or disability insurance.

If you are unsure if you are compliant in this area, now is a good time to refresh your memory by reviewing the compliance expectations in their entirety by visiting the Advertising channel in InfoSight.

InfoSight also offers a advertising checklist to use at your discretion.

Industry News

FHA adds 40-Year Mortgage Modification Option
The Federal Housing Administration announced that it is adding a new, 40-year modification option for mortgage servicers to use in conjunction with its partial claim option to assist additional borrowers who are behind on their mortgage payments for FHA Title II forward mortgages. This new loss mitigation home retention option is designed to help those borrowers who cannot achieve a minimum targeted 25 percent reduction in the Principal and Interest portion of their mortgage payment through FHA’s existing 30-year mortgage modification with a partial claim.

FHFA Finalizes Strategic Plan
The Federal Housing Finance Agency issued the FHFA Strategic Plan: Fiscal Years 2022-2026, which provides a framework that outlines the Agency’s priorities for the coming years as regulator of the Federal Home Loan Bank System and as regulator and conservator of Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac (the Enterprises).

The Strategic Plan continues existing priorities and formalizes areas of focus for FHFA and its regulated entities by establishing three goals:

  • Secure the regulated entities’ safety and soundness
  • Foster housing finance markets that promote equitable access to affordable and sustainable housing
  • Responsibly steward FHFA’s infrastructure

Compliance Q&A's

Q: Can a phone be used as an access device for Reg E? Is there a way that we can ask the member if they have a passcode to access their phone?

A: A phone may function as an access device when it is properly equipped. This is often done with software and remember an access device includes a “code” which is input into the software to provide access to an account. Even if the member has a passcode that does not mean that the member is automatically liable for the transaction. An investigation is still required.

Just as you teach your members not to write their PIN on the card or to keep it with their card, your credit union should be advising consumers to use multifactor authentication and biometrics to protect their accounts. We have heard claims of members allowing others to use their phone and later find out funds were transferred from an account, or a person may sell their phone and not completely erase confidential information that is somehow accessed. Credit unions should be using examples such as these to caution and protect their members because if not, a credit union is setting itself up for future claims. Your members need periodic safety reminders to help protect them from loss or theft.

Q: During a routine audit we identified that a teller was force balancing her drawer. The overages or shortages were never substantial amounts. Do we need to file a SAR?

A: While a SAR is required for insider abuse regardless of dollar amount, an investigation should be conducted to determine if this was truly embezzlement, theft or disappearance of funds or merely an employee who was afraid of the repercussions for not balancing the teller drawer. If the latter, appropriate disciplinary action should be taken, but a SAR would not be required.

Q: We have been filing continuing SARs on a business for money laundering. The business has now closed, and the activity no longer continued. Are we required to notify FinCEN that the activity ceased?

A: No. There is not a regulatory requirement to notify FinCEN or file a final SAR to notify FinCEN that suspicious activity has ceased.
