CU Action Update

Adverse Action Notices Under Equal Credit Opportunity Act
Common Adverse Action Errors
With so much focus currently on making new loans, there are bound to be errors during this process. Errors can be associated with withdrawn, incomplete, or denied applications and there can also be errors due to the lack of accurate and timely adverse action notices. Failing to provide an adverse action notice (AAN) within the required timeframe is quite common. This happens more frequently when an application is incomplete, or a counteroffer has been made. Timing requirements are governed under Regulation B, you can learn more by using the link provided.

Under Regulation B, a creditor is required to notify an applicant of an adverse action within:

  • Thirty days of receiving a complete credit application.
  • Thirty days after receiving an incomplete credit application.
  • Thirty days after taking action on an existing credit account.
  • Ninety days after making a counteroffer on an application for credit if the applicant does not accept the offer.

To meet these deadlines, it is important to review your current procedures for weaknesses to determine if changes need to be made.

Another common error is the failure to send an AAN due to the application incorrectly being considered withdrawn. Keep in mind, withdrawn applications do not require AAN if the applicant notifies the credit union verbally or in writing of their intent to withdraw before the applicable deadline. So, when an applicant does not respond to contact attempts that is not considered a withdrawn application.

If you are unable to make a decision because you need more information, you have the option to deny the loan for incompleteness and issue an AAN within 30 days of application or provide a notice of incompleteness verbally or in writing notifying the applicant that additional information is needed within 30 days of the application.

If you decide to provide a notice of incompleteness, your notice should include the information needed, a deadline to provide information and they must be made aware that the application will not be considered further if the requested information is not provided. If you do not hear back from the applicant by the deadline, you may consider the application closed for incompleteness and no further action is required by you.

Another area of confusion is when a lender confuses a withdrawn application with a denied one, this is seen most frequently when a counteroffer is not accepted. Consider this example, you receive an application for a fixed loan rate for a 30-year term. After reviewing the application & credit, the credit union determines that the applicants score does not meet the minimum requirements for this product. However, they do qualify for an ARM loan for a 20-year term. The credit union contacts the applicant and provides the counteroffer.

If the applicant does not accept the 20-year ARM loan offer, the application is denied and an AAN notice is required based on the original application. This is due to the fact that the applicant did not qualify for the original terms they applied for and did not accept the counteroffer.

SIDE NOTE - The regulation imposes two notice requirements for counteroffers. First the creditor must send a notice with the terms of the counteroffer within 30 days after receiving a completed application. Second, if the applicant does not accept or use the credit offered within 90 days of the initial counteroffer notice, then the creditor must send an AAN for the counteroffer. To help reduce this burden you are allowed to combine the AAN for the original application along with the counteroffer, provided the combined notice is sent with 30 days after receiving the completed application. If you choose to use the combined notice, and the applicant does not respond within 90 days, the creditor is not required to send a second AAN. It is good business practice to include an expiration date for the counteroffer, the regulation does not require that you hold a counteroffer open for any particular period of time. That is a business decision.

Adverse Action Compliance Tips

  • If a combined notice is not used, make sure you have controls in place to ensure that an AAN is sent out if the applicant does not accept the counteroffer within 90 days.
  • A counteroffer should not be mistakenly treated as an approval and an AAN must be provided, if applicable.
  • If your credit union obtains sufficient information to make a credit decision and denies the application, the application should not be treated as withdrawn because the applicant does not inquire about the status of it. An AAN is required because the application was denied.
  • Make sure to sufficiently train staff to communicatee with applicants about loan products to avoid compliance risk associated with handling inquires.
  • Insufficient internal controls for monitoring third parties that are used for an AAN may elevate compliance risk

Industry News

CFPB Acts to Protect Consumers with Government Benefit Accounts
Recently, the CFPB issued Compliance Bulletin 2022-02 on the Electronic Fund Transfer Act’s Compulsory Use Prohibition and Government Benefit Accounts.

The bulletin confirms that EFTA’s consumer protections apply to government benefit accounts, and financial institutions may be held liable for violations of this requirement. The bulletin also confirms that it is a violation of law when people are not provided a choice on where to receive their first payment, even if they can redirect subsequent payments to an account of their choice.

Regulatory Alert - 22 Truth in Lending Act (Reg Z) Adjustment to Asset-size Exemption Threshold
The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) published annual adjustments for asset-size exemption thresholds under the Truth in Lending Act (TILA), as implemented by Regulation Z, which became effective on January 1, 2022. The asset-size thresholds exempt certain credit unions from escrow account requirements for higher-priced mortgage loans and specific qualified mortgages under Regulation Z. Reg Z: Escrows and Small Creditor Qualified Mortgages – Asset Size Exemption Threshold Increased to $2.336 Billion
Creditors with assets of less than $2.336 billion (including assets of certain affiliates) on December 31, 2021, are exempt from the requirement to establish escrow accounts for higher-priced mortgage loans in 2022 if other provisions of Regulation Z are also met. This asset-size threshold also applies during a grace period, in certain circumstances, with respect to transactions with applications received before April 1, 2023.

Georgia Residential Mortgage Act (GRMA) Per-Loan Fees
