CU Action Update

Treasury Targets Sanctions Evaders and Russian Tech Companies
The Treasury Department recently announced that OFAC has designated 21 entities and 13 individuals as part of its crackdown on the Kremlin’s sanctions evasion networks and technology companies, which are instrumental to the Russian Federation’s war machine. Treasury has also determined that three new sectors of the Russian Federation economy are subject to sanctions pursuant to Executive Order 14024. This allows Treasury to impose sanctions on any individual or entity determined to operate or have operated in any of those sectors. OFAC also designated Russian government malicious cyber actors.

Compliance Q and A's

Clarification of Savings Bond FAQ
The following FAQ was included in the April 6, 2022, Compliance eNewsletter.

Q: Is my credit union required to cash/redeem savings bonds for nonmembers?

A: If you are a Federally chartered credit union, you are an authorized paying agent of U.S. savings bonds. As an authorized paying agent, your FCU is required to redeem savings bonds for nonmembers when requested to do so according to 31 C.F.R 321.10. Please keep in mind that although you are acting as a paying agent of the U.S. government this does not mean you are required to cash other checks issued by the federal government for nonmembers.

Clarification
Federal (or State) Credit Unions are not automatically authorized paying agents of U.S. Savings Bonds. They are eligible to become paying agents. Unless a Federal or State Credit Union applies to become a paying agent, they are not required to cash savings bonds for either members or nonmembers.

The citations and links listed below may be helpful in understanding the distinction between eligibility and becoming an authorized paying agent:

Q: To what aspects of the loan process does Reg B (Equal Credit Opportunity Act) apply?

A: Reg B affects the prescreening process and advertising as well as taking, evaluating and deciding on a credit application. It also affects furnishing and maintaining credit information.

Q: If a credit union has policies and procedures that prohibit discrimination against any of the protected groups (sex, marital status, etc.), does it need to be concerned any further with discrimination under the rule?

A: Yes. Reg B also provides that creditors may be held liable if their credit-related actions have the effect of discriminating, through disparate impact, even if the creditor did not purposefully mean to discriminate. For example, requiring applicants to have a certain income level to get overdraft protection for share drafts could mean that minority applicants would be rejected more often than non-minority applicants. However, if the credit union can demonstrate that a certain income level is a reasonable requirement for the level of credit sought, the requirement will be permissible.

Q: Does Reg B mean that credit unions may not use pertinent information to evaluate creditworthiness?

A: No. Credit unions may determine any pertinent information necessary to evaluate an applicant's creditworthiness, as long as the credit union does not illegally discriminate in the credit transaction or application process.

Q: In taking the application, may a credit union ask about the applicant's spouse?

A: Yes, but only in limited situations: When the spouse will be a user of the account (such as a credit card account) or joint obligor; the applicant is relying on the spouse's income to repay the debt; the applicant is in a community-property state or relies on property in such a state for repayment; or the applicant is relying on alimony, child support or maintenance as a source of income for repayment.

Q: What about asking about marital status while taking an application?

A: A credit union may ask about marital status only if the application is for joint credit; the applicant offers property or assets as security for the credit or when the applicant resides in community- property state or relies on property in such a state as a basis for repayment. Inquiries about marital status are limited to whether the applicant is "married," "unmarried" or "separated."

Q: Should the application ask whether the applicant is male or female?

A: Generally, not, but it may provide for the designation of a title, such as Mr. or Ms. if it indicates that the designation is optional. Of course, questions about child-bearing intentions are prohibited. The credit union may ask about the applicant's ability to repay, such as the probability of continuing employment if this question is asked of other applicants similarly qualified. The number, ages and expenses of present children may also be requested.

Q: What about the requirement that certain loan applications must include information about race or national origin, sex, etc.?

A: Reg B contains a seeming contradiction. On the one hand, creditors generally are not allowed to ask about sex or marital status, except as indicated above. However, for loans to purchase or refinance the applicant's principal dwelling (including mobile homes and condos), the lender is required to take the application in writing and request the following information: race or national origin, sex, marital status, and age. This information is used by the federal regulators to monitor compliance with Reg C. Written applications are not required under Reg B for other types of loans.

Industry News

NCUA to Begin Phase 2 of Resuming Onsite Operations
In July 2021, the NCUA announced the implementation of Phase 1 of its phased approach to returning to onsite operations in Letter to Credit Unions, 21-CU-06. Based on new guidance from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) and the Safer Federal Workforce Task Force, the agency will enter the second phase (Phase 2) of resuming its onsite operations on April 11, 2022. The NCUA has issued Letter to Credit Unions, 22-CU-06 with further explanation.

Phase 2 permits NCUA staff to volunteer to work onsite, including conducting examination and supervision work at credit unions located in counties with low or moderate COVID-19 community levels, as defined by the CDC. Onsite work in counties with high COVID-19 community levels may be allowed when necessary and with prior approval from NCUA management.
