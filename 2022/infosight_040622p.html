CU Action Update

Savings Bond FAQs
Savings bonds are not instruments we hear much about anymore compared to the years I spent working in the branches. However, lately we have had credit union suffer losses due to improper handling. Savings bonds like all other check cashing instruments have compliance expectations and it is important that your frontline staff is trained properly to prevent losses. Use the FAQs below as a refresher and a resource for training.

Q: Is the credit union responsible for issuing the 1099-INT for Savings Bonds redeemed by both their members and non-members?

A: Yes, if the bond is redeemed at a credit union, it is responsible for issuing the 1099-INT. Individuals who contact the Treasury about their 1099-INT are directed to the credit union’s 1099 department to request a duplicate 1099-INT be sent.

Q: Is my credit union required to cash/redeem savings bonds for nonmembers?

A: If you are a Federally chartered credit union, you are an authorized paying agent of U.S. savings bonds. As an authorized paying agent, your FCU is required to redeem savings bonds for nonmembers when requested to do so according to 31 C.F.R 321.10. Please keep in mind that although you are acting as a paying agent of the U.S. government this does not mean you are required to cash other checks issued by the federal government for nonmembers.

Q: A member’s father/mother recently passed away and it was discovered that he/she owned several bonds. Can we redeem these bonds for the member?

A: If only one person is named on a savings bond, and that person is deceased, his or her bond is the property of his or her estate. "Estate" as used here means any belongings or other property left by the person who is deceased.

If both people named on a bond are deceased, the bond is the property of the estate of the person who died last. Once the bonds become the property of the estate, a court order dated within the last 12 months must be presented by the executor before they can be redeemed.

Retain both death certificates and a copy of the letters of appointment for the representative. Redemption requests may also be sent to the Treasury Retail Securities Site at the Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis for processing.

If one of two people named on a bond is deceased, the surviving person is automatically the owner just as if that survivor had been the sole owner from the time the bond was issued. A copy of the death certificate is required.

Once it has been determined who the bond is being cashed for, it will be necessary to go to the bond web site to obtain form to be completed and sent to the government for cashing. These bonds cannot be cashed at the credit union. For additional information on bonds and access to the necessary forms go to www.savingsbonds.gov

Q: Can a bond be redeemed by a beneficiary?

A: Yes, a surviving beneficiary listed on a bond may redeem the bond using normal identification procedures if they present a copy of the death certificate. Redemption requests may also be sent to the Treasury Retail Securities Site at the Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis along with a completed FS Form 1522 (Off-site, PDF) Special Form of Request for Payment of United States Savings and Retirement Securities Where Use of a Detached Request Is Authorized.

Q: Can my credit union establish guidelines around when we will redeem a savings bond?

A: Financial institutions are required to cash savings bonds or notes that are eligible for payment for an established account holder at their credit union who presents the proper identification and who seems worthy of their trust.

Payment to a person who doesn’t hold an account at the credit union or who only recently opened an account is at the option of the financial institution. Refer to The Guide to Cashing Savings Bonds (Off-site, PDF) for details.

Q: Under what circumstances can we deny the redemption of a savings bond?

A: There are circumstances under which you can refuse to redeem a bond. For example, if a bond appears to have been defaced or altered in any way. A good rule of thumb is, if you don’t feel comfortable with the validity of the bonds or are suspicious of the individual presenting them, do not cash the bond. Forward it to the Treasury Retail Securities Site at the Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis for processing. Things to look for include:

  • Ensure there are no variations in font and type size
  • Check to be sure the printed area of the bond is aligned LEFT
  • Ensure there are no variations in alignment, which indicate changes
  • Check for serrated edges on newer bonds. Many older bonds do not have serrated edges

Q: We recently suffered a financial loss due to the redemption of savings bonds. Is there a tool I can provide my tellers to help them through the process?

A: Periodic training is always the best tool for your staff. Savings bonds are not something that staff may see frequently, so this area may require more training than others. Use the online training tool linked below to help them determine if they should redeem the bond or not. This would also be a good link for them to use outside of training.

Georgia Credit Unions

GA Credit Unions Receive Letter from Department of Revenue
On May 5, 2021, the Request for Financial Institution Information for Delinquent Taxpayers, GA Code 48-2-63 under the Georgia Department of Revenue went into effect. No guidance or instruction on how financial institutions were to implement this was shared prior to the distribution of this. Just recently, LSCU found out that the Department of Revenue sent out a cover letter along with a Memorandum of Agreement (MOA) here to all financial institutions addressed to the CEO or Compliance Officer. We are working with other financial institution trade associations and the Georgia Department of Banking and Finance to clarify some of the language in the MOA. The LSCU Compliance and Regulatory Services and Advocacy team will be putting out a Compliance Bulletin with guidance and clarification one we have it from the Department of Revenue. Stay tuned.

Industry News

FBI and International Partners Stop Wire Transfer Schemes
The FBI recently conducted a coordinated effort to disrupt Business Email Compromise (BEC) schemes that intercept, and steal wire transfers sent by businesses and individuals. BEC is a sophisticated scam that often targets employees of businesses that make payments through wire transfers. Skilled scammers usually gain access to a company’s email accounts or spoof their email addresses to send legitimate sounding and well-timed requests for wire transfers. The bank accounts provided for the receipt of transfers, however, are controlled by the criminals. The same criminal organizations that carry out BEC also exploit individuals, often real estate purchasers and the elderly.

Action Plan for Fighting Bias in Appraisals
The Interagency Task Force on Property Appraisal and Valuation Equity ("PAVE") has issued an Action Plan [Executive Summary; Full Plan] to root out racial and ethnic bias in home valuations. The PAVE task force was directed by President Biden to evaluate the causes, extent, and consequences of appraisal bias and to establish a set of recommendations to eliminate racial and ethnic bias in home valuations. Thirteen federal agencies participated in the task force.
