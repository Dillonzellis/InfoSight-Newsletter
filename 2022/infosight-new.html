Senator Presses Treasury Secretary on Collection, Sale of Financial Data

Senate Banking, Housing, and Urban Affairs Committee Chairman Sherrod Brown (D-OH) has written to Treasury Secretary Janet Yellen, who oversees the Financial Stability Oversight Council (FSOC), to express concern with the collection and sale of consumer financial data practices.

Brown’s concerns focused on bad actors obtaining and using private consumer data for their own purposes and profit, especially given the extent of personal data financial institutions can access, as noted.

“The breadth of personal consumer data that financial institutions have access to and can legally sell or otherwise disclose to commercial entities and to data brokers creates a concerning entry point for bad actors to obtain and use that information for their own purposes,” wrote Brown. “As chair of FSOC, you have identified risks to our financial system posed by cybersecurity incidents, including ransomware attacks and data breaches. In a similar vein, I encourage FSOC to assess whether and to what extent the collection and sale of consumer data by financial institutions pose a systemic threat to the country’s financial stability,” he concluded.

Question and Answer

Q:What is succession planning?

A:Succession planning is a key component in preparing for the future of the credit union. It is a systematic process ensuring leadership continuity by helping credit unions prepare for planned or unplanned absences of the CEO, director, or other critical staff positions. An effective succession plan will enable the credit union to keep critical operations going by maintaining stability and continuity of operations, retaining “institutional knowledge,” and minimizing disruption during periods of change. In short, a credit union's strategic plan is not sound without the inclusion of a viable succession plan.

Q:Why is a strategic plan a necessary part of succession planning? Isn’t succession planning about people?

A: Succession planning should include not only planning to replace people, but should be strongly coordinated with organizational planning. It should begin with an understanding of a company’s strategic direction and practices – the credit union’s vision of where it will be going in the future – and it should be tied to the management of all the transitions that the credit union is now facing, or will one day face. The Board, the CEO, and senior management need to anticipate the knowledge, skills, and abilities needed to meet the credit union’s strategic goals.

Q:Should the President/CEO/Manager be the only focus of succession planning?

A:The board should be planning for multiple successions. While the first succession plan developed should be for the CEO role, the board should also plan for the boards’ own succession as well as for other “key” positions that are critical to the success of the credit union

Q:What “key” or “critical” positions should be included in succession planning?

A:Succession planning should focus on any key position that is critical to the credit union’s operations, including those that are critical to the overall organizational structure of the credit union.

Examples include:

  1. Key tasks that would stop critical action from taking place;
  2. specialized or unique expertise that is difficult to replace; and
  3. a position that may be the only one of its kind in a particular geographical location in which would be difficult for another location to carry out its functions.

 

Q:Shouldn’t each department within the credit union be responsible for its own succession planning?

A:Continuity works best. Allowing each department to come up with its own unique process for succession planning can be a troublesome and time-consuming endeavor. Credit unions should create a company-wide process that can be used by each individual department.

Q:Is succession planning only a one-time event? Once a succession plan is drafted, why is it necessary to update and/or revise it?

A:The succession plan should be a living, breathing document that is updated at least annually to account for strategic redirections and changes. It is important to think about succession planning as an ongoing process to continually grow and develop the credit union, which includes continual adjustment to strategic redirections and changes, external market changes, and internal training and development.
